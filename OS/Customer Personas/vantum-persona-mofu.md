# Persona — The Systemized Veteran

**Funnel Stage:** MOFU · Warm Prospect
**Buying Window:** Conditional — waiting for a trigger event

---

## Snapshot

| | |
|---|---|
| **Role** | Fractional CFO, COO, CTO, CMO, CRO (often hybrid) |
| **Tenure** | 5–15 years independent |
| **Client Load** | 3–6 active engagements |
| **Weekly Hours** | 40–55. Saturdays mostly free. |
| **Team** | 0 to 1 part-time VA |
| **Tool Spend** | $300–$1,500 / month |
| **Decision Power** | Sole. Heavy internal justification. |
| **Annual Revenue** | $250K–$800K |
| **Buying Window** | Conditional — waiting for a trigger |

---

## Background

They've been running their fractional practice for years. They have named pricing tiers. They have a productized offer. They have a refined Notion workspace they've tuned over a decade and a custom GPT they've nicknamed something charming — "Techie" or some equivalent. They have spent the last five-plus years building scaffolding around the chaos that fractional work creates — and they have, by most measures, succeeded.

Their Saturdays are mostly free. They have plenty of time. They've solved capacity through AI and experience, and they will tell you so within the first ten minutes of conversation.

But the structural problem is still there. They just have language for it: *"It's a structural thing, but I've figured it out."* On the weekends when they should be off, they spend six hours rebuilding their Notion or tuning their custom GPT. Their conversion rate is great. Their pipeline cycles are manageable. Their own business goals... well, those are running on a longer arc than they used to. They've stopped tracking them quarterly. They look back annually now. Sometimes biannually.

**They love what they do. They are proud of how they do it.** And every six to nine months, something — a sixth client lands, the GPT breaks, a use case appears that their stack can't handle — and they spend a weekend rebuilding. They tell themselves this is the cost of doing business. It's not wrong. It's also not free.

---

## A Tuesday and a Saturday

The week looks fine. The weekend is where the cost shows up.

- **Tuesday, 9:00 AM** — Notion opens. The week looks reasonable. Three deliverables for two retainer clients, a sales call at 2 PM, and a Saturday morning slot blocked off for working on a new pricing tier they've been meaning to launch.
- **Tuesday, 12:00 PM** — Deliverables mostly done. The custom GPT pulled together the morning briefing in eight seconds. The system works.
- **Tuesday, 5:00 PM** — Sales call taken. CRM updated. They close the laptop satisfied. They tell their partner, "Today was a good day."
- **Saturday, 9:30 AM** — They sit down at the laptop. Coffee. They open Notion to start on the pricing tier. Something feels disorganized. The properties aren't right. The database view needs a rethink. They start reorganizing.
- **Saturday, 12:42 PM** — The Notion workspace is now beautifully reorganized. They have written zero words about pricing. They tell themselves they'll start next Saturday.

They will not. The pricing tier has been six months out for fourteen months.

## Goals

- Maintain the lifestyle they designed — 40–55 hour weeks, Saturdays mostly free
- Extend their capabilities without spending more hours *(the exact phrase they use)*
- See cross-project priority across all clients in one view
- Protect the high-margin work; quietly drop the low-margin work
- Move their own pricing, positioning, productization forward — without burning a Saturday
- Show their peers they've solved this

---

## Frustrations (named, but minimized)

- The custom GPT was supposed to solve everything. It solved some things.
- Cross-project chaos — "what's next, what's late, what's waiting"
- Meeting people in the wrong order — losing relationship opportunities to memory failure
- "Doing for others what I don't do for myself"
- The 4th, 5th, 6th client makes the system creak — but they don't see it until weeks after
- Tool fatigue — they've migrated Notion → ClickUp → back to Notion in two years
- The weekend rebuilds. They don't count them as work.

## In their words

> "I created an agent called Techie to help me manage the five to ten tech stacks I'd have to try to learn. I don't know what my cap is anymore — I'm at 6 clients and still exploring."

> "We want a partner that's going to know the way we roll and what makes us special — not something that flattens 35 years of experience into a template."

> "Of the five projects I'm running — what is where, what is my next best action, what is waiting, what is late? I need a super integrated project management list across the whole ecosystem."

> "I would like to find a way to extend my capabilities and my service delivery without having to spend more hours on it."

> "I'm having a hard time getting out of my own way, doing for me what I do for others."

> "I meet people in the wrong order. I usually don't meet them when I need them — I meet them before I need them."

> "My goal is not to sell you my hours. My goal is to put value on your P&L."

## Their stack today

Years of tuning. Each tool added deliberately. Each integration custom. The stack is the artifact of their craft — and any pitch that implies it should be replaced will be rejected on contact.

- Custom GPT (named — "Techie" or similar)
- Refined Notion or ClickUp workspace, multi-year tuning
- Mature CRM (HubSpot, Pipedrive)
- HoneyBook for invoicing
- Named pricing tiers / productized offer on their website
- Part-time VA (some) for inbox and admin
- Cal.com or Google Calendar with extensive availability rules
- Loom library
- Internal docs nobody else can navigate
- Slack with custom workflows

The right framing: **Vantum runs on top of this, not instead of it.** The wrong framing — and the one most SaaS pitches default to — is "replace your stack." They will close that tab immediately.

---

## What makes them look

Nurture content does not convert them. A breakdown event does. The job of marketing is to be the first thing they remember when their own system fails.

- **The 6th or 7th client lands** — even the optimized system cracks
- **A peer they trust** (preferably running 4+ clients themselves) names the problem in their language
- **A weekend rebuild costs them their entire Saturday** — and they realize it's recurring
- **A use case appears the custom GPT can't handle** — multi-client capacity forecasting, cross-project priority, relationship CRM
- **A pricing crisis** where they need to see capacity vs. revenue clearly — and can't, even with their system
- **An annual review** showing their own business goals slipped despite the scaffolding

---

## What they need to believe

- This is **not a generic PM tool** — it understands the fractional reality specifically
- It **respects their craft** — integrates with their custom GPT instead of replacing it
- Other **senior fractionals (10+ years)** are already using it, not just first-time consultants
- ROI is measured in **reclaimed own-business hours**, not "save time" platitudes
- They can try it **without ripping out their existing stack**
- It **will not flatten** their 10–35 years of experience into a template
- A **real fractional**, not a SaaS founder, built this

## How to talk to them

### Headlines

- **Comparison:** "Vantum vs. Notion: when DIY hits its capacity ceiling."
- **Pattern:** "The 4th client problem — and why most fractionals don't see it coming."
- **Case study angle:** "I built my own GPT. Then I built Vantum on top of it."
- **Quarterly touchpoint:** "Did your own business move this quarter? A 5-minute audit."
- **Webinar:** "How 10+ year fractionals are upgrading from custom GPTs to integrated capacity tooling."

### Objection handling

| They say | You answer |
|---|---|
| "I built my own system." | So had every customer with a custom GPT. Want to see how three of them now use Vantum *with* their GPT, not instead of it? |
| "I'm not overwhelmed." | You're not. Your system is doing its job. The question is: what happens when client #6 lands and adds three new Workspaces? |
| "I'm too senior for that." | It's not for the junior operator. It's for the operator running 4+ clients who's already burned through Notion, Asana, ClickUp. |
| "Show me ROI in dollars." | Here's the model: every protected own-business hour at your billing rate. We track first protected hour by week 1. |

### Nurture rule

This persona does not buy on the call. They buy 30–90 days after the call, when their system breaks and they remember you. **Job of marketing:** be present, be respected, and be the first vendor they DM when the Saturday rebuild happens. Pitching now loses them. Planting flags wins them.

---

## Where to find them

They read more than they watch. They attend webinars but skip demos. They follow founders, not products.

**Reach them on:**
- Substack / LinkedIn long-form
- Founder essays
- 30-min webinars
- Fractional executive communities (Fractionals United, Chief)
- Peer-led case studies
- Quarterly review touchpoints
- Podcasts (as host, not guest — they want to be the expert)
- Industry conferences (selective)

**Where they are NOT receptive:** Cold outreach, paid ads, mass nurture emails, anything that pattern-matches to "vendor selling to me."

---

## Who is not this persona

- **The exhausted 4-client operator** who hasn't built a system yet and is willing to admit it — that's BOFU, sell now
- **The lifestyle operator** running 25 hours by choice with no system pain — that's TOFU, awareness only
- **The advisor** who doesn't deliver hands-on — strategic-only work doesn't generate the structural pain
- **The first-year fractional** still figuring out pricing — they don't have the scar tissue yet
- **The full-time employee** with a "fractional CFO" title at an accounting firm — disqualified at the role level
- **The operator between engagements** — their problem is pipeline, not capacity
