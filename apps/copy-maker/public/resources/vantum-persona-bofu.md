**Funnel Stage:** BOFU · Bullseye Buyer
**Buying Window:** Now. Would pay tomorrow.

## Background

They left a VP-level role at a growth-stage company about three years ago. They wanted control over their time and their work. They've gotten control over the work — clients pay them well, the engagements are interesting, the resume is strong. The time has been eaten alive.

By client three, they'd built a Notion. By client four, the Notion was a mess and they migrated to Asana. By client five, they were running a custom GPT, scheduling emails at 2 AM to hit Canadian client inboxes, and quietly suspecting that none of the tools were the problem.

They are good at the work. Clients are happy. But the business *they* founded — the one with their own name on it — is suffering. The website they meant to launch last year is still "coming soon." The outbound campaign they keep scheduling for next Tuesday keeps getting pushed to the Tuesday after. Their own content cadence collapsed two quarters ago. They tell themselves they're not lazy. They're right. They are over capacity and reactive.

**They admit all of this when you ask directly.** That admission is what separates them from the operators who look like them on paper but won't buy. Their pain is humble — "it's a me problem" — not blameful. They believe the problem is their own discipline. It is not. It is structural. And once they hear that framing from someone who has lived it, they listen.

## A Tuesday in their week

By Wednesday, none of this will have happened.

- **8:14 AM** — Laptop open in the home office. Five client inboxes stacked across five Google Workspaces. A note from Client A's CEO marked URGENT, sent at 7:53 AM. They'd planned to spend Tuesday on a long-overdue content sprint for their own LinkedIn.
- **8:30 AM** — On a Zoom with Client A's team. The "urgent" is real. They block their afternoon to draft a response strategy.
- **11:00 AM** — Reshuffling the entire week in Asana. The content sprint moves to Thursday. They suspect Thursday will also disappear.
- **4:00 PM** — Drafting deliverables for Client A instead of their own content. They notice they haven't eaten.
- **11:13 PM** — Scheduling emails to land in international inboxes the next morning. They remember they also told a friend they'd review their pitch deck this week. They haven't started their own LinkedIn post. They won't.

This pattern repeats. They know it does. They have been telling themselves "next week" for six quarters.

---

## Goals

- Protect at least one focused block of own-business work per week, no matter what client fire hits
- Onboard a 4th or 5th client without losing the system that holds the previous three together
- See their real capacity before they say yes to new work, so they can price it or decline without guilt
- Stop the slow drift toward being a pure delivery shop
- Move their own goals — content, pricing, the website — forward in measurable increments
- Sleep at night without rehearsing tomorrow's to-do list

---

## Frustrations

- The week breaks by Wednesday. Always.
- Five different client Workspaces, each with its own tools they have to live inside
- Tool migration fatigue — Notion → Asana → ClickUp → custom GPT → spreadsheets
- They cannot say what their actual capacity is. They only know when they're over it.
- They work weekends to "catch up" — and what they catch up on is their own work, not the clients
- The self-criticism loop: "Is the client really upset, or am I making this bigger than it is?"
- They haven't lost a client yet. They believe they will. The feeling never goes away.

> "The client could only afford me Mondays and Tuesdays, but they wanted me seven days a week. And I was super flexible — maybe that was my error."

## Their stack today

A stitched-together collection. None of it built for someone serving five clients at once. Each tool was added in response to a specific moment of pain, and each is now part of a system that almost works.

- Google Calendar (5+ Workspaces, used as the master hub)
- Notion or Asana (currently — will probably migrate again in 6 months)
- Custom GPT or Claude
- HoneyBook for invoicing
- Slack across multiple client workspaces
- A spreadsheet stitched between systems they cannot otherwise connect
- LinkedIn for their own marketing, when they have time
- Email scheduling tool for off-hours outbound
- A Loom or screen-record habit
- Zoom / Google Meet

They have already migrated once. They are halfway through migrating again. They suspect — correctly — that the tool is not the problem.

---

## What makes them look

They are not browsing for tools. They are not on G2. They are in their inbox. The moment they go from "I'll get to it" to "I need to fix this" is almost always a specific event.

- A breakdown event in the last 30 days — a missed deadline, a 70-hour week, a quarter where their own goals visibly slipped
- Onboarding a 4th or 5th client when current tools are clearly cracking
- The third consecutive quarter of "I'll start outbound next month"
- A near-loss of a client forcing a pipeline rebuild from scratch
- Another tool migration looming — and finally realizing the tool isn't the problem
- A peer they trust naming the pattern in language they recognize

---

## What they need to believe

- The problem is **structural** — not their work ethic. Running 3+ clients solo breaks any system without specific scaffolding.
- This tool was **built by someone who has personally run 4+ clients** themselves.
- It **respects what they've already built** — their GPTs, their calendar, their notes — and doesn't ask them to start over.
- Other fractionals **running the same load** are already using it.
- ROI shows up **in a single quarter**, not six months.
- It **will not flatten** their experience into a generic PM template.

## How to talk to them

### Landing page H1 (primary)
"You know exactly what you should be working on this week. You also know you won't get to it."

### Landing page H1 (alternate)
"When the client fires hit, Vantum replans your week. So the work that matters most doesn't slip — it moves forward."

### Demo opener
"Walk me through your last broken week."

### Email subjects
- "How's your week holding up?" *(Wednesday afternoon)*
- "Q3 closed. Did your own business move?" *(quarter-end)*
- "The 4th client problem"

### Objection handling

| They say | You answer |
|---|---|
| "I've already built my own system." | So had every customer of ours. Show me yours — most lose to the first big client fire of the quarter. |
| "Notion works fine." | Notion is great with one client. What happens when client #4 has a different Workspace, different tooling, different priority? |
| "I don't have time to learn another tool." | Onboarding is 20 minutes. The first week pays it back in protected own-business hours. |
| "I just need to be more disciplined." | That's what every fractional says before the third migration. Discipline doesn't scale across five Workspaces. Scaffolding does. |

## Who is not this persona

People who look similar in a LinkedIn search filter but won't buy:

- The operator with 6 clients who insists they've "solved capacity with AI"
- The 10+ year veteran who says "I have plenty of time"
- The operator who designed for 35 hours by choice and is genuinely happy there
- The person currently between engagements with no active retainers
- The strategy-only advisor who doesn't deliver hands-on
- The full-time CFO at an accounting firm using "fractional" as a job title
- The aspiring fractional who hasn't taken on a client yet

Disqualify on hard filters (active strain, active revenue) before spending demo cycles.
